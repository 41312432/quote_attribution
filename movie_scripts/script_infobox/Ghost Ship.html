JTucker (6 out of 10 )
Ghost Ship utilizes the very effective premise of "discovering the abandoned ship where something horrible has happened"- similar to Alien and Event Horizon. The story is gripping throughout its first 3/4's. It stalls because none of the characters have enough baggage/back story to propel them to take action on their own. Epps' escape is aided by a ghost whom has no reason to save her except out of altruism- which is not foreshadowed in any set-up earlier in the story. The flaw that sinks "Ghost Ship" is the failure to reveal "WHY" the ship is evil. What set the actions in motion to make everyone go crazy? Without this key explanation there is no completion to the story. A character "in the know" would have helped. A treasure hunter motivated by the "El Dorado" of salvage work who knows the legend or story of Chimera would help. He would naturally lie and hide details leading his crew into unforeseen danger out of greed or megalomania. He could be motivated in a similar way as "Ahab" in Moby Dick. This character would be strong enough for Epps to play off of and take action on a moral level that would make her character stronger. The opposition needs to be real and dynamic. This is what deflates the final tension and conflict as wholly predictable and unsatisfying. Human psychology requires an explanation to feel as though a story is complete. These weaknesses prevent "Ghost Ship" from becoming a timeless horror picture.
